# Arizona ALPR Accountability Field Brief

## Purpose

This brief turns the Arizona anti-Flock / camera-accountability campaign into a source-backed civic workflow. It is designed for residents, technologists, privacy advocates, journalists, students, city-council watchers, campus communities, and FLLC members who want public safety without private mass tracking.

## Bottom line

Arizona should not normalize private ALPR grids without public proof.

A city or campus should not buy or keep an ALPR network unless residents can see the contract, camera locations, retention rules, audit logs, sharing partners, warrant policy, and renewal calendar. Public safety should be measurable, limited, and accountable.

## What Flock / ALPR systems do

An automated license plate reader system generally captures an image of a vehicle, extracts or records the license plate number, stores the time and location, and compares the scan against hotlists or search criteria. Depending on the vendor, contract, and integrations, the system may also include vehicle descriptions, direction of travel, network sharing, alerting, video products, or adjacent public-safety analytics.

Do not overstate unknown capabilities. Use source-backed language:

- Supported: ALPR systems can create searchable vehicle-location records.
- Supported: contracts and policies can define retention and sharing.
- Supported: misuse and over-sharing risks exist and have been reported.
- Needs verification before claiming: face recognition, audio capture, driver identity, or live behavioral scoring at a specific location.

## Traffic England and the public data warning

The Traffic England closure notice says the Traffic England website is closing on 30 June 2026 and points the public toward navigation providers such as Google, Waze, and TomTom, National Highways pages, and professional NTIS data access.

That matters because public-road information is civic infrastructure. If direct public portals close and private systems become the main interface, residents may lose independent visibility into public-road conditions and public-camera operations. Arizona should preserve public-access road data and not let road intelligence become only vendor-mediated.

## AZ511 connection

AZ511 is Arizona's public traveler-information surface. It represents the public-side model: road status, traffic information, alerts, and camera visibility when available.

The campaign should defend and improve public road information:

- public camera layers should remain public where lawful and safe
- public road-status feeds should stay accessible
- traffic cameras should be distinct from ALPR surveillance
- private vendors should not become the only practical way to understand public roads
- public data should be documented, versioned, and easy to cite

## Public safety test

Before supporting any ALPR deployment, ask:

1. What problem is being solved?
2. What less invasive alternatives were considered?
3. What data is collected?
4. How long is data retained?
5. Who can search it?
6. What outside agencies can access it?
7. Is a warrant required?
8. How are misuse and false hits handled?
9. What are the annual metrics?
10. When does the contract expire?

If the agency cannot answer, the deployment is not ready.

## Minimum ordinance language

A serious ALPR ordinance should include:

- public registry of camera locations
- mandatory published contract
- maximum retention period
- role-based access control
- case-number requirement for searches
- audit logs retained for public review
- external sharing only with warrant or court order
- written notice to the city before vendor disclosure
- prohibition on sale of resident data
- annual transparency report
- independent audit
- sunset date
- public renewal vote
- misuse penalties

## Public message

Short version:

> Public safety should not become private mass tracking.
>
> If Arizona cities, campuses, HOAs, or police departments use Flock or any ALPR system, residents deserve the contract, camera locations, retention policy, audit logs, sharing partners, warrant rules, and annual public vote.
>
> No secret camera grids. No hidden data sharing. No sale of resident location data.
