# Legislation Impact Framework

## Purpose

This framework explains how Overture reviews legislation, agency policy, public contracts, and civic technology decisions. The goal is to show how a rule moves from paper into systems that affect residents.

The core question:

> What does this rule permit in practice, and what documents prove how it works?

## Impact chain

```text
Authority
  -> funding
  -> procurement
  -> implementation
  -> data collection
  -> access rules
  -> retention rules
  -> sharing rules
  -> operational use
  -> correction process
  -> public reporting
  -> renewal or sunset
```

Every layer should have a source.

## 1. Authority

Identify the source of power.

Questions:

- Is the authority from state law, city ordinance, agency policy, campus policy, contract, grant, or court rule?
- Who sponsored, approved, or signed it?
- What problem was stated in the public record?
- What limits were written into the rule?

Evidence:

- bill text
- ordinance text
- committee minutes
- council agenda
- staff report
- agency policy
- meeting video

## 2. Funding

A policy becomes real when money is attached.

Questions:

- What budget line funds the system?
- Is the money local, state, federal, grant-based, or vendor-financed?
- Does the funding source create reporting duties?
- Does funding continue automatically?

Evidence:

- budget documents
- grant applications
- award letters
- invoices
- purchase orders
- contract amendments

## 3. Procurement

Procurement records show what was actually purchased.

Questions:

- Was there a competitive bid?
- What vendor was selected?
- What features were included?
- What features were optional?
- What renewal terms apply?
- Who owns the records produced by the system?

Evidence:

- request for proposal
- bid response
- scoring sheet
- contract
- statement of work
- renewal amendment
- sole-source justification

## 4. Implementation

Implementation determines how the system actually works.

Questions:

- Where is the system used?
- What devices, databases, dashboards, feeds, or integrations exist?
- Who administers user accounts?
- What training is required?
- What audit trails are generated?

Evidence:

- deployment list
- device inventory
- user manual
- administrator policy
- training records
- integration documents

## 5. Data collection

Public systems should define exactly what they collect.

Questions:

- What fields are collected?
- Are timestamps, locations, images, identifiers, metadata, alerts, searches, or exports stored?
- Is any data inferred or enriched?
- Are records linked across systems?

Evidence:

- data dictionary
- technical sheet
- contract exhibit
- retention schedule
- privacy policy
- vendor security document

## 6. Access control

Access rules define who can use the system.

Questions:

- Which public employees can access it?
- Which contractors or vendor staff can access it?
- Are outside organizations allowed access?
- Is each search tied to a documented purpose?
- Are access logs reviewed?

Evidence:

- access policy
- user-role table
- audit log sample
- outside-access agreement
- memorandum of understanding
- training materials

## 7. Retention and deletion

Retention defines how long records remain useful.

Questions:

- What is the default retention period?
- Are there exceptions?
- Are exports retained separately?
- Are backups retained longer?
- Are audit logs retained?
- Is deletion documented?

Evidence:

- retention schedule
- deletion policy
- backup policy
- contract terms
- records-management policy

## 8. Sharing and disclosure

Sharing rules determine whether a local system stays local.

Questions:

- Who can receive records?
- What approval is required?
- Are requests logged?
- Does the local agency receive notice before disclosure?
- Can the vendor disclose records directly?
- Can records be sold, licensed, aggregated, or used for unrelated purposes?

Evidence:

- data-sharing agreement
- request policy
- vendor disclosure clause
- public-records response
- annual report

## 9. Operational use

Operational use is where policy becomes action.

Questions:

- What decisions are made from the system?
- Is there human review before action?
- Are results treated as leads or final proof?
- What standard is required before escalation?
- Are users disciplined for misuse?

Evidence:

- standard operating procedure
- training document
- disciplinary policy
- audit report
- incident review
- complaint records

## 10. Error correction

Every public system needs a correction path.

Questions:

- How are incorrect results identified?
- Who can challenge an error?
- How quickly must errors be corrected?
- Are affected residents notified when appropriate?
- Are recurring problems reported publicly?

Evidence:

- complaint process
- incident review procedure
- correction log
- vendor support terms
- annual error report

## 11. Public reporting

Public reporting makes a system reviewable.

Questions:

- Is there an annual public report?
- Are searches, alerts, disclosures, errors, complaints, and misuse events reported?
- Are reports machine-readable?
- Is the public told before renewal?

Evidence:

- annual transparency report
- audit report
- public dashboard
- meeting agenda
- renewal staff report

## 12. Renewal or sunset

Every high-impact public system should have a review point.

Questions:

- When does the contract expire?
- Does the policy sunset automatically?
- Is there a public hearing before renewal?
- Can residents review performance metrics before renewal?
- What happens if the vendor changes terms?

Evidence:

- contract renewal clause
- sunset clause
- council calendar
- procurement calendar
- amendment history

## Minimum safeguards to request

- published contract
- public device inventory where lawful
- clear purpose limitation
- exact data-field list
- short retention period
- deletion proof
- role-based access
- documented-purpose requirement
- access logs
- independent audit
- resident complaint process
- false-result review process
- written outside-access rules
- vendor disclosure restrictions
- no sale of resident data
- annual transparency report
- sunset clause
- public renewal vote

## Maintenance rule

Do not treat a policy win as permanent. Contracts renew, vendors change features, state laws change, and agencies revise policies. Every issue tracked by Overture should have a review date.
